Activity 1.2.6.

Jalen needs groceries, so rides his bike to the store and it takes him half an hour. After finishing his shopping, he sees his friend Alex who offers him a ride home. He takes the same route home as he did to the store, but this time it only takes one-fifth of an hour. If his average rate was \(18\) mph faster on the way home, how far away does Jalen live from the grocery store?
We’ll use the subscript \(b\) to refer to variables relating to Jalen’s trip to the store while riding his \(b\)ike and the subscript \(c\) to refer to variables relating to Jalen’s trip home while riding in his friend’s \(c\)ar.
(a)
How long does his bike trip from home to the store and his car trip from the store back home take?
  1. \(t_b=18\) hours, \(t_c=18\) hours
  2. \(t_b=\frac{1}{5}\) of an hour, \(t_c=\frac{1}{2}\) of an hour
  3. \(t_b=\frac{1}{2}\) of an hour, \(t_c=\frac{1}{5}\) of an hour
  4. \(t_b=2\) hours, \(t_c=5\) hours
  5. \(t_b=5\) hours, \(t_c=2\) hours
(b)
Write an equation relating \(r_b\) and \(r_c\text{.}\)
  1. Both the trip to the store and the trip home covered the same distance, so \(r_b=r_c\text{.}\) Since they are the same, we can just call them both \(r\text{.}\)
  2. We don’t know how the two rates relate to each other, so cannot write an equation comparing them and must leave them as separate variables \(r_b\) and \(r_c\text{.}\)
  3. Jalen’s rate on the trip home in the car was \(18\) mph faster than his trip to the store on his bike, so if we let \(r_b=r\text{,}\) then \(r_c=r-18\text{.}\)
  4. Jalen’s rate on the trip home in the car was \(18\) mph faster than his trip to the store on his bike, so if we let \(r_b=r\text{,}\) then \(r_c=r+18\text{.}\)
(c)
Fill in the following table using the information you’ve just determined about the Jalen’s rates and times on each leg of his grocery store trip. Then fill in the distance column based on how distance relates to rate and time in each case.
(d)
Our goal is to figure out how far away Jalen lives from the store. To help us get there, write an equation relating \(d_b\) and \(d_c\text{.}\)
  1. The distance he traveled by bike is the same as the distance he traveled by car, so \(d_b = d_c\)
  2. The distance he traveled by bike took longer than the distance he traveled by car, so \(d_b + \frac{1}{2} = d_c + \frac{1}{5}\)
  3. The distance, \(d\text{,}\) between his house and the grocery store is sum of the distance he traveled on his bike and the distance he traveled in the car, so \(d_b + d_c = d\text{.}\)
  4. The distance, \(d\text{,}\) between his house and the grocery store is sum of the difference he traveled on his bike and the distance he traveled in the car, so \(d_b - d_c = d\text{.}\)
(e)
Now plug in the expressions from your table for \(d_b\) and \(d_c\) into the equation you just found. Notice that it is a linear equation in one variable, \(r\text{.}\) Solve for \(r\text{.}\)
(f)
Our goal was to determine the distance between Jalen’s house and the grocery store. Solving for \(r\) did not tell us that distance, but it did get us one step closer. Use that value to help you determine the distance between his house and the store, and write your answer using the context of the problem. (Hint: can you find an expression involving \(r\) that we made that represents that distance? )

  2. The grocery store is 8 miles away from Jalen’s house.
  3. The grocery store is 10 miles away from Jalen’s house.
  4. The grocery store is 12 miles away from Jalen’s house.
  5. The grocery store is 14 miles away from Jalen’s house.
